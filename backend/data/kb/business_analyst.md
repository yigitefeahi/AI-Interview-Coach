# Business Analyst — Interview Coaching Knowledge Base

## Role overview

Business analyst interviews assess requirements discovery, process analysis, stakeholder management, and translation of business needs into buildable specifications. Success is measured by fewer rework cycles, clearer acceptance criteria, and shipped solutions that meet validated goals—not length of documentation.

## Core competencies

- Elicitation: interviews, workshops, observation, document analysis
- Requirements: user stories, acceptance criteria, use cases, traceability
- Process modeling: AS-IS/TO-BE flows, BPMN basics, bottleneck analysis
- Prioritization: MoSCoW, RICE, value vs effort, dependency mapping
- Validation: UAT planning, test scenarios, success metrics, sign-off
- Tools: Jira, Confluence, SQL/Excel for light analysis, wireframe collaboration

## Interview themes

Case prompts: gather requirements for ambiguous initiative, resolve conflicting stakeholders, improve manual workflow, define acceptance criteria, validate post-launch outcomes. Behavioral questions cover pushback, scope creep, and working with engineering when specs were wrong.

## Question bank

**Case:** New customer dashboard requested by sales and finance—approach? Two executives want conflicting priorities. Manual invoice process takes 3 days—improve it. Write acceptance criteria for password reset flow.

**Behavioral:** Requirement changed mid-sprint. Stakeholder bypassed you to dev team. UAT found critical gap—response?

**Technical light:** SQL to validate business rule. Sample user story breakdown for epic.

## Strong answer framework

Identify stakeholders and goals → current pain (quantified) → proposed solution scope → requirements and out-of-scope → acceptance criteria and test cases → rollout/validation plan → success metrics. Show facilitation, not only documentation.

## Strong answer signals

- Quantifies pain: hours wasted, error rate, revenue delay
- Separates problem from prescribed solution in requirements
- Acceptance criteria are testable, not vague "user friendly"
- Documents assumptions and gets explicit sign-off
- Follows up post-launch with metric check vs business case

Example strong fragment: "Finance needed T+1 reporting but ops entered data T+2. I mapped the handoff, found duplicate Excel step causing 6-hour delay. We scoped API export MVP with four acceptance tests. Reporting SLA met for 95% of days within two sprints."

## Weak answer signals and red flags

- Writes specs without talking to users or ops
- Cannot prioritize; everything is P0
- Acceptance criteria like "system should be fast"
- Blames developers when validation was weak
- No measurable success definition

## Common interviewer follow-ups

- What did you explicitly leave out of scope?
- How do you handle changing requirements?
- How would you test this without engineering help?
- Difference between requirement and solution?
- How do you say no to a senior stakeholder?

## Seniority signals

**Junior:** Documents under guidance. **Mid:** Facilitates workshops, owns BA stream for feature. **Senior:** Drives cross-functional discovery, process redesign, and benefits realization tracking.

## Coaching tips

Use one conflict resolution story and one UAT catch story. For bank or telecom BAs, emphasize compliance checkpoints and audit trails in requirements. Show empathy for both business urgency and engineering constraints.
## Example answer snippets

**Weak:** "I gathered requirements and wrote documents."
**Strong:** "Call center handle time rose 20%. I shadowed agents, mapped AS-IS flow, and found duplicate CRM lookup. TO-BE flow with auto-populated customer ID scoped to two sprints with five acceptance tests. Average handle time fell 14% post-UAT."

## Local market context

Large Turkish enterprises (telecom, banks) have formal UAT and compliance gates. Show facilitation skills in Turkish stakeholder workshops and traceability from business goal to test case.
